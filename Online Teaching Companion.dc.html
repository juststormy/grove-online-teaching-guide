A Guide to Effective Online Teaching

Your students are out there. Let's make sure they can feel you in the course.

Seven research-backed principles, adapted for online science courses — with room to reflect honestly, a place to plan, and one small thing to try before your next module opens. This isn't a checklist for perfection. It's a set of lenses.

Imagine a student in your Genetics course. She's at her kitchen table in Pittsburgh at 10:30 p.m. Her kids are finally asleep. She works full time. She hasn't been on a campus in years. She is genuinely trying to understand meiosis — and she is doing it completely alone.

She's not an edge case. She's your student.

Peer review of online teaching

The seven principles in this companion aren't only a lens for your own reflection — they're the shared framework Penn State uses to review online teaching. Peer review of teaching, like peer review of research, is a recognized way to promote and document quality, and it's required for promotion and tenure.

Looking for something lower-stakes?

This page is the formal, summative review that goes in your annual report or P&T dossier. If you'd rather have a voluntary, confidential observation partnership — for your own reflection, never sent to anyone — that's a separate resource, and it covers both online and face-to-face courses.

Explore Peer Observation Partnerships →

The instrument below — the Peer Review Guide for Online Teaching at Penn State — was developed by Ann Taylor at the John A. Dutton Institute (College of Earth and Mineral Sciences), built on the same Seven Principles, and approved for University-wide use by the Penn State Online Coordinating Council. This companion is adapted from that work.

Three ways to use it
Formal review

A colleague reviews your course and summarizes their observations in a letter for your annual review or P&T dossier (Policy AC23).

Informal review

Ask a trusted colleague to give you low-stakes feedback on one part of your teaching you're actively working on.

Self-review

Use it on your own course to see clearly what you're shooting for — exactly what you've been doing in this companion.

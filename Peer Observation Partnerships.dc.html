Peer Observation Partnerships

Teaching happens behind a closed door.

Whether you teach in a lecture hall or entirely in Canvas, almost no colleague ever sees you do it. A peer observation partnership opens that door on purpose — volunteer to pair with another instructor, watch how each other teaches, and gather honest data to reflect on and improve, entirely on your own terms.

Why partner at all?

Teaching is the one thing we do entirely alone, in a room no colleague ever sees.

Even in a building, a colleague rarely sees more than a glimpse through an open door — and online it never happens at all. Most of us teach on the evidence of a grade distribution and an end-of-term survey. A partner changes that, in person or online — and the research on why is surprisingly strong.

Before anything else

What this is — and what it isn't.

This is not the formal peer review that goes in your dossier. It's a separate, voluntary, developmental partnership. The distinction matters, so here it is plainly.

What it isn't

Nothing from this partnership goes to your chair or the dean. The notes, the data, the conversation — they are yours to keep, share, or set aside, and no one else's to see.

Need the formal, summative review for your annual report or P&T dossier instead? That's a separate process with its own instruments — find it in the online teaching companion.

How it works

A reciprocal cycle, paced to your semester.

You set the focus. Your partner gathers data against it. You make sense of it together — then you switch seats and do the same for them.

Two ways to observe

However you teach, there's a way to be seen.

Observation isn't one-size-fits-all. Pick the mode that matches how this course actually meets — a live classroom or an online course in Canvas. The partnership cycle is identical; only the way your partner gathers data changes.

Face-to-face In the room · powered by CCEDIR

Live classroom observation

A partner joins one of your class sessions and uses the CCEDIR protocol — Capturing Classroom Engagement Data for Instructor Reflection — to log what's happening minute by minute: when you're presenting, when students are working, when discussion is live. Afterward you receive a spreadsheet and turn it into a visualization of your own engagement patterns — the kind of snapshot that's almost impossible to see from inside the moment.

Online In Canvas · the Seven Principles lens

Online course observation

There's no class session to sit in on, so your partner is given guest or TA access to your online course and reviews it asynchronously, on their own time. Working from the Seven Principles of effective online teaching, they look at how you show up in the course and where students might be quietly slipping through — then bring it to your debrief.
